## üèõÔ∏è DIPLOMATIC & POLITICAL ADVISORY ROLES

### Core Concept
Students are advisors, diplomats, or decision-makers who must analyze historical precedents to guide current policy.

**Fresh Scenarios:**

**Treaty Negotiation (1648, Treaty of Westphalia)**
- You are a Swedish diplomat. 30 Years War is ending. How should territorial concessions be structured based on: Peace of Augsburg (1555), Congress of Vienna principles, medieval imperial boundaries?

**Cold War Crisis Management (1962, Cuban Missile Crisis)**
- You advise JFK. Soviet missiles in Cuba. Options: blockade, airstrike, invasion, negotiation. What do historical precedents suggest? Berlin Blockade (1948), Korea (1950), Suez Crisis (1956) outcomes?

**Decolonization Strategy (1947, India)**
- You advise Mountbatten on partition. Analyze: Ottoman Empire breakup (1918), Irish partition (1921), peaceful vs. violent independence movements. What minimizes bloodshed?

**Alliance Formation (1778, Franco-American)**
- Benjamin Franklin seeks French support vs. Britain. You advise Louis XVI: What happened when France supported rebellions before? Analyze costs/benefits of Seven Years' War, earlier colonial interventions.

## ‚öñÔ∏è LEGAL & JUDICIAL CONTEXTS

### Core Concept
Students analyze historical legal precedent, constitutional interpretation, or justice systems evolution.

**Fresh Scenarios:**

**War Crimes Tribunal (1945-46, Nuremberg)**
- You are a prosecutor. Which documents prove "crimes against humanity"? Analyze: Hague Conventions, Geneva Conventions, precedent from earlier war trials. What evidence is admissible?

**Constitutional Crisis (1803, Marbury v. Madison)**
- You are Chief Justice Marshall. Does Supreme Court have power to void laws? Examine: British legal tradition, Federalist Papers arguments, colonial charter interpretations. What historical precedent exists?

**Indigenous Land Rights (Modern, but historical evidence)**
- You are a judge. Tribe claims treaty violation from 1851. Analyze: original treaty language, historical maps, comparable cases (Cherokee Nation v. Georgia 1831), evolving property law concepts.

**Slavery Abolition Legal Strategy (1840s-1850s)**
- You are an abolitionist lawyer. Which legal arguments have historically succeeded in undermining slavery? Analyze: Somerset case (1772 England), Haitian precedent, state-by-state abolition patterns.

## üéñÔ∏è MILITARY STRATEGY & COMMAND DECISIONS

### Core Concept
Students are commanders who must apply historical military strategy to current tactical situations.

**Fresh Scenarios:**

**Siege Warfare (1529, Vienna vs. Ottomans)**
- You command Vienna's defense. Enemy has numerical superiority but extended supply lines. What strategies worked in: Constantinople (1453), Rhodes (1522), earlier Hungarian battles? Rank options: sally, attrition, negotiation, relief force.

**Naval Strategy (1805, Trafalgar)**
- You are Nelson's flag captain. French-Spanish fleet is superior in ships. What unconventional tactics succeeded historically? Analyze: Salamis (480 BCE), Lepanto (1571), previous British naval victories.

**Guerrilla Warfare Response (Vietnam War, 1960s)**
- You advise US military. Viet Cong use guerrilla tactics. What counter-insurgency methods succeeded/failed historically? Analyze: British in Malaya (1950s), French in Algeria (1954-62), Spanish guerrillas vs. Napoleon (1808-14).

**Combined Arms Tactics (1940, Fall of France)**
- You are a French general. Germans mass tanks at Ardennes. Historically, how have defenders countered armor concentrations? Analyze: WWI anti-tank tactics, Spanish Civil War lessons, Polish September campaign (1939).

## üè∫ MUSEUM, ARCHAEOLOGY & ARTIFACT AUTHENTICATION

### Core Concept
Students are experts determining authenticity, age, or cultural significance of historical objects.

**Fresh Scenarios:**

**Painting Authentication (Potential Caravaggio, modern day)**
- Christie's may pay ¬£5 million. Painting shows: chiaroscuro lighting, religious martyrdom, oil on canvas, frame dated 1750s. Is this 1600s Caravaggio or later forgery? Compare to verified Caravaggio works, Baroque techniques, aging patterns.

**Ancient Manuscript Verification (Dead Sea Scrolls, 1947)**
- You evaluate scrolls found in Qumran caves. Carbon dating suggests 100 BCE - 100 CE. Do text styles, language usage, paleography match this period? Compare to: verified Aramaic texts, Hebrew script evolution, known 2nd Temple period writings.

**Viking Artifact Analysis (Vinland Map controversy)**
- Map appears to show Norse America discoveries pre-Columbus. Parchment is medieval, but ink chemistry is disputed. Analyze: Norse sagas, archaeological evidence from L'Anse aux Meadows, medieval mapmaking techniques, known forgery patterns.

**Egyptian Artifact Provenance (Modern museum acquisition)**
- Statue claimed to be Dynasty 18 (1550-1295 BCE). Features include: particular blue pigment, hieroglyphic spelling variations, artistic style. Does this match New Kingdom characteristics? Could it be modern forgery? What tests are conclusive?

## üóûÔ∏è JOURNALISM & HISTORICAL FACT-CHECKING

### Core Concept
Students are journalists/fact-checkers verifying historical claims or providing historical context.

**Fresh Scenarios:**

**Documentary Accuracy Check (Vietnam War documentary, modern)**
- Film claims Gulf of Tonkin incident (1964) was "manufactured." You must verify. Analyze: declassified NSA documents, McNamara memoirs, contemporary news reports, Naval communications logs. What does evidence support?

**Political Speech Fact-Check (Modern politician citing historical parallel)**
- Candidate compares current situation to "Munich 1938 appeasement." Is comparison accurate? Analyze: actual Munich Agreement terms, Chamberlain's alternatives, historical consensus on appeasement, differences from current situation.

**Anniversary Retrospective (Fall of Berlin Wall, 1989)**
- Writing 30th anniversary piece. What were actual causes vs. popular myths? Analyze: Gorbachev's policies, East German economic crisis, Hungarian border opening, contemporaneous documents vs. later narratives.

**Historical Claim Verification (Columbus "discovery" narrative)**
- Fact-checking textbook. Does evidence support "Columbus discovered America"? Examine: Norse Vinland settlements (~1000 CE), Indigenous presence (15,000+ years), definition of "discovery," historiographical evolution of this claim.

## üìö ACADEMIC RESEARCH & HISTORIOGRAPHY

### Core Concept
Students are historians evaluating sources, constructing arguments, or analyzing how history has been interpreted.

**Fresh Scenarios:**

**Primary Source Analysis (newly declassified CIA documents, 1953 Iran)**
- Documents reveal Operation Ajax details. How does this change understanding of 1953 Iranian coup? Compare: earlier historical accounts, British role, Shah's position, consequences for US-Iran relations.

**Competing Historical Narratives (Causes of WWI)**
- Analyze 3 historical interpretations: A) German aggression (Versailles view), B) System failure (alliance rigidity), C) Miscalculation/accidents. What primary evidence supports each? Which is most convincing?

**Oral History Evaluation (Holocaust survivor testimony)**
- You're collecting testimonies for Shoah Foundation. How do you corroborate personal accounts? What details can be verified? How do memory, trauma, and time affect reliability? Compare multiple witness accounts of same events.

**Historiographical Shift Analysis (Slavery in US history)**
- How has slavery interpretation evolved? Compare: 1900s-1950s narratives, 1960s-70s revision (incorporating slave voices), 1619 Project reframing. What new sources drove each shift?

## üí∞ ECONOMIC HISTORY & POLICY ANALYSIS

### Core Concept
Students analyze historical economic policies, crises, or trade systems to inform current decisions.

**Fresh Scenarios:**

**Financial Crisis Response (2008 crash)**
- You advise Treasury Secretary. Should response mirror: Great Depression (1929) New Deal policies, Japan's Lost Decade (1990s) approach, or 1907 banking panic resolution? What worked/failed in each case?

**Trade Route Control Strategy (1580s, Elizabethan England vs. Spain)**
- Advise Elizabeth I. Spanish control: Gibraltar Strait, Manila galleons, Antwerp. Which English actions would maximize disruption? Analyze: Portuguese-Dutch conflicts, Venetian-Genoa trade wars, historical chokepoint seizures.

**Currency Debasement Decision (Roman Empire, 3rd century CE)**
- You advise Emperor. Military costs soar, tax revenue falls. Debase silver content of denarius? Analyze: previous debasement consequences, inflation patterns, empire-wide economic effects, comparison to other civilizations facing this choice.

**Colonial Resource Extraction Model (1600s-1800s)**
- You evaluate different colonial economic systems: Spanish encomienda, Dutch VOC mercantilism, British Atlantic trade. Which generated most sustainable wealth? What were long-term consequences for colonized regions?

## üèõÔ∏è INFRASTRUCTURE & URBAN PLANNING (HISTORICAL)

### Core Concept
Students make infrastructure decisions as historical leaders, weighing costs/benefits based on historical outcomes.

**Fresh Scenarios:**

**Roman Public Works (312 BCE)**
- You are a consul. Senate funds ONE project: A) Via Appia road (military mobility), B) Aqua Appia aqueduct (urban water), C) City walls (defense), D) Circus Maximus (political support). Analyze Roman priorities, current threats, comparable Greek city decisions. Rank by strategic value.

**Medieval Bridge Construction (1100s-1200s)**
- You govern a growing trade city. Build expensive stone bridge over major river? Analyze: London Bridge success, Florence's bridges, toll revenue models, military vulnerability of bridges, maintenance costs vs. ferry alternatives.

**Industrial Revolution Urban Planning (1840s Manchester)**
- Factory smoke pollutes, disease spreads in slums, workers demand sanitation. Fund: A) Sewage system, B) Parks/open spaces, C) Worker housing, D) Wider streets. What did other industrial cities do? What worked?

**Trans-Siberian Railway Decision (1890s Russia)**
- You advise Tsar. Railway costs are enormous. Benefits: Siberian development, military mobility to Asia, trade route to Pacific. Analyze: US transcontinental railroad outcomes, Indian railway impact under British rule, cost-benefit for Russian empire.

## ‚öîÔ∏è REVOLUTION & SOCIAL MOVEMENT ANALYSIS

### Core Concept
Students analyze revolutionary situations, predict outcomes, or advise on conflict resolution based on historical patterns.

**Fresh Scenarios:**

**Revolutionary Crisis Management (Russia 1905, "Bloody Sunday")**
- Advise Tsar Nicholas II. Workers demand parliament, rights. Options: repression, limited reform, full democratization, economic concessions only. What happened when others faced similar choice? Analyze: France 1789, Britain 1832, Germany 1848, Meiji Japan.

**Independence Movement Strategy (India 1920s-40s)**
- You advise Indian National Congress. British rule entrenched. Compare strategies: Gandhi's non-violence vs. Bose's armed resistance. What worked in: Irish independence, Haitian revolution, Latin American independence movements?

**Counter-Reformation Response (1517, Luther's 95 Theses)**
- Advise Pope Leo X. German monk challenges indulgences. Options: ignore, excommunicate, negotiate, use force. What happened with: Cathars (crusade), Hussites (partial success then suppression), Wycliffe (died naturally but followers persecuted)? What unintended consequences?

**Decolonization Violence Prevention (1960s Africa)**
- You're UN mediator. Colonial power withdrawing, ethnic tensions high. How can you prevent Rwanda-like violence? Analyze: India-Pakistan partition disaster (1947), Algeria's bloody independence (1954-62), Ghana's peaceful transition (1957). What factors explain differences?

### Core Concept
Students apply historical public health responses to disease outbreaks or demographic challenges.

**Fresh Scenarios:**

**Pandemic Response (COVID-19 vs. 1918 Spanish Flu)**
- You advise government on lockdown policy. Compare: 1918 city responses (Philadelphia parade disaster vs. St. Louis early closure), quarantine effectiveness in historical plague outbreaks, economic consequences of different approaches. What does history suggest?

- Military recruitment falls, tax base shrinks, plague recurs. Options mirror historical responses: settle barbarian foederati (integration risk), incentivize Roman births (expensive), expand citizenship (devalue status). What did other empires facing depopulation do?

## üìå KEY PRINCIPLES FOR ALL SCENARIOS

### ‚úÖ ALWAYS INCLUDE:

1. **Specific Date/Period** - Not "ancient Rome" but "312 BCE, consulship of Appius Claudius Caecus"
2. **Named Historical Actors** - Real people, not "a king" but "King Ferdinand II of Aragon"
3. **Concrete Options** - "A, B, C, D" choices based on actual historical alternatives
4. **Multiple Historical Parallels** - Compare 2-4 analogous situations from different times/places
5. **Primary Source References** - Treaties, documents, archaeological evidence by name
6. **Measurable Consequences** - "5 million lives lost" not "many died"; "20-year war" not "long conflict"

- Industrialization (Britain, Germany, Japan, USA variations)

- Decolonization across Africa, Asia
- Cold War (multiple regional conflicts)
