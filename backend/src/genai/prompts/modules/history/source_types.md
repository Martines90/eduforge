## üéØ CORE HISTORICAL KNOWLEDGE CATEGORIES

History education develops **5 interconnected knowledge domains**:

### 1. üìÖ LEXICAL KNOWLEDGE (Who, What, When, Where)
**What students must know:**
- **Dates & Chronology**: Specific dates, time intervals, phases of processes (year/decade/century precision)
- **Events**: Battles, revolutions, campaigns, reforms, treaties, discoveries
- **Actors**: Countries, states, empires, rulers, military commanders, key figures, social movements
- **Locations**: Strategic cities, borders, trade routes, battle sites, affected regions
- **Political & Economic Processes**: Wars, alliances, economic policies, trade systems

**Real-world application**: "You need to negotiate with Emperor X. When did they rise to power? What major events shaped their worldview?"

---

### 2. üîó PROCESS & CAUSATION (Why Things Happened)
**What students must understand:**
- **Cause-Effect Relationships**: What triggered events? What were the consequences?
- **Multiple Causation**: How did economic, political, social, and cultural factors interact?
- **Long-term vs. Immediate Causes**: Underlying conditions vs. triggering events
- **Intended vs. Unintended Consequences**: What was planned? What actually happened?

**Real-world application**: "Why did Revolution X succeed while Revolution Y failed? What were the critical differences?"

---

### 3. üëë HISTORICAL TRAJECTORIES (Rise, Peak, Decline, Fall)
**What students must analyze:**
- **Career Paths of Leaders**: Rise to power ‚Üí At peak ‚Üí Gradual weakening ‚Üí Sudden fall
- **Empires & Nations**: Flourishing ‚Üí Stagnation ‚Üí Decline ‚Üí Complete destruction
- **Political Systems**: Formation ‚Üí Consolidation ‚Üí Crisis ‚Üí Transformation or collapse
- **Economic Cycles**: Growth ‚Üí Peak ‚Üí Recession ‚Üí Recovery or collapse

**Real-world application**: "Emperor X is at their peak now. Based on similar historical figures, what threats should you anticipate?"

---

### 4. üè∫ ARCHAEOLOGICAL & MATERIAL EVIDENCE
**What students must evaluate:**
- **Artifacts**: Tools, weapons, art, architecture, coins, inscriptions
- **Physical Evidence**: Supporting or challenging historical theories
- **Dating Methods**: Determining age and authenticity
- **Cultural Context**: What objects reveal about societies

**Real-world application**: "This artifact has features A, B, C. Which century and civilization does it belong to?"

---

### 5. üìñ PRIMARY SOURCE ANALYSIS
**What students must interpret:**
- **Document Types**: Letters, treaties, laws, speeches, diaries, newspapers, government records
- **Author Perspective**: Who wrote it? Why? What bias might they have?
- **Historical Context**: What was happening when this was created?
- **Reliability Assessment**: How trustworthy is this source?

**Real-world application**: "You're analyzing Treaty X. What does the language reveal about the power dynamics between the parties?"

---

## üéØ THE HISTORICAL INQUIRY FRAMEWORK

Every history task should enable students to answer:

### **WHO / WHAT?**
- Which actors? (Countries, states, persons, institutions, social groups)
- What interests did they represent?
- What alliances existed?

### **WHEN?**
- Specific event date
- Interval of an ongoing process
- Phases of the process
- OR estimate: yearly, decade, or century precision

### **WHERE?**
- Important locations
- Affected areas
- Strategic geographic points
- Trade routes, borders, regions

### **WHY?**
- Causes (economic, political, social, cultural, ideological)
- Connections and their explanations
- Multiple perspectives on causation

### **HOW?**
- Methods, strategies, tactics employed
- Process by which change occurred
- Mechanisms of power, control, influence

### **WHAT WERE THE CONSEQUENCES?**
- Immediate vs. long-term effects
- Intended vs. unintended outcomes
- Winners and losers
- Historical legacy

### 1. ü§ù DIPLOMATIC NEGOTIATION & ALLIANCE BUILDING

**Core Scenario**: You are a diplomat/high-ranking official preparing for critical negotiations

**Historical Knowledge Required**:
- Past treaties, alliances, betrayals between these parties
- What worked/failed in similar historical situations
- Power dynamics, military strengths, economic leverage
- Cultural values, religious beliefs affecting negotiation

**Example Task Structure**:
> "You are a French diplomat in 1778. Benjamin Franklin seeks French military alliance against Britain. What risks exist based on past British-French conflicts?"

**Question Types**:
- List advantages of peace/alliance/economic cooperation (backed by historical examples)
- What guarantees/concessions should be offered? (based on past treaties)
- Which historical alliance model is most applicable?
- What mistakes from past negotiations should be avoided?

- Effectiveness of different weapons/formations in this context

**Example Task Structure**:

**Question Types**:

- Which tax policy caused revolt in [comparable situation]? How can you avoid this?
- Compare economic outcomes of policy X (in country A) vs. policy Y (in country B)
- What unintended consequences occurred when [historical ruler] implemented [tax policy]?

## üéØ TASK DESIGN PRINCIPLES FOR HISTORY

### ‚úÖ EVERY HISTORY TASK MUST:

1. **Place student in AUTHENTIC ROLE** requiring historical knowledge
   - Diplomat, ruler, military commander, judge, curator, journalist, researcher
   - NOT "student studying history" - actual professional using history

2. **Create GENUINE NEED** for historical analysis
   - Critical decision with consequences
   - Problem that ONLY historical knowledge can solve
   - Real-world application (policy, law, strategy, authentication, etc.)

3. **Require SPECIFIC HISTORICAL KNOWLEDGE**, not vague generalities
   - Exact dates, events, figures, documents
   - Precise geographic locations
   - Concrete examples from actual history

4. **Demand COMPARISON & ANALYSIS**, not just recall
   - Compare multiple historical examples
   - Identify patterns across cases
   - Evaluate outcomes of different historical approaches
   - Apply lessons to new situations

5. **Provide PRIMARY SOURCES or detailed historical evidence**
   - Actual excerpts from treaties, letters, speeches, laws
   - Archaeological evidence descriptions
   - Statistical data from the period
   - Multiple perspectives on the same event

6. **Show CONSEQUENCES** of historical knowledge application
   - What happens if you choose wrong alliance?
   - What if you misdate the artifact?
   - What if you ignore historical precedent?
