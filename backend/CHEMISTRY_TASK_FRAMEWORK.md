# CHEMISTRY TASK FRAMEWORK

## Overview

This document outlines the comprehensive framework for generating authentic, scenario-based chemistry tasks that place students in **professional roles facing real-world chemical challenges**.

**Core Philosophy**: Students are NOT "studying chemistry" - they ARE chemists, engineers, toxicologists, historians, or other professionals **using chemistry to solve critical problems**.

## üéØ Design Principles

### 1. Professional Authenticity
- **Real Roles**: NASA engineer, forensic toxicologist, mining engineer, conservation chemist, alchemist
- **Real Decisions**: Life-critical calculations, historical investigations, industrial safety
- **Real Consequences**: Mission failure, poisoning deaths, environmental disasters, historical mysteries

### 2. Specific Historical/Contemporary Context
- **Exact Dates**: April 14, 1970 (not "1970s"), November 20, 2006 (not "2000s")
- **Exact Locations**: Lakehurst, New Jersey; Pripyat, Ukraine; Valley of the Kings, Egypt

## üìö Four Core Knowledge Domains

### 1. üìñ LEXICAL KNOWLEDGE
**Definitions, Concepts, Rules, Terminology**

What students need to **recognize and define**:
- **Chemical Terminology**: Stoichiometry, molarity, pH, half-life, oxidation state, equilibrium
- **Fundamental Constants**: Avogadro's number, gas constant R, Faraday constant F

- **Classification**: Acids/bases, types of reactions (synthesis, decomposition, combustion, redox)
- **Nuclear Chemistry**: Radioactive decay, half-life, alpha/beta/gamma radiation, fission

**Example Tasks**:
- Define LD50 in context of cyanide poisoning (Gold Rush scenario)
- Explain half-life using Litvinenko Polonium-210 case
- Identify acid-base reaction in Cleopatra's pearl dissolution
- Define stoichiometry using Apollo 13 CO‚ÇÇ scrubber calculation

### 4. ‚öóÔ∏è CHEMICAL COMPOSITION, INTERACTIONS & TRANSFORMATIONS
**Reactions, Kinetics, Equilibrium, Energy Changes**

What students need to **predict and calculate**:

#### Chemical Reactions
- **Types**: Synthesis, decomposition, combustion, neutralization, redox, precipitation
- **Balancing**: Stoichiometric coefficients, conservation of mass
- **Yield**: Theoretical, actual, percent yield calculations
- **Example**: Hindenburg combustion 2H‚ÇÇ + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí 2H‚ÇÇO

#### Reaction Kinetics & Rates
- **Rate Laws**: Effect of concentration, temperature, catalysts
- **Half-life**: Radioactive decay (Polonium-210, Iodine-131, Cesium-137)
- **Activation Energy**: Catalysis in industrial processes
- **Example**: Chernobyl graphite fire rate, Stradivari varnish polymerization time

#### Chemical Equilibrium
- **Le Chatelier's Principle**: Stress on equilibrium systems
- **Equilibrium Constants**: Kc, Kp, Ka, Kb, Ksp
- **Buffer Systems**: pH control in biological/industrial systems
- **Example**: Cave formation CaCO‚ÇÉ + H‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ ‚áå Ca(HCO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ

#### Energy & Thermochemistry
- **Enthalpy Changes**: ‚àÜH for combustion, neutralization, dissolution
- **Bond Energy**: Breaking/forming bonds in reactions
- **Hess's Law**: Calculating ‚àÜH from multi-step pathways
- **Calorimetry**: Heat capacity, specific heat, temperature changes
- **Example**: Hindenburg H‚ÇÇ combustion energy (‚àÜH = -286 kJ/mol), Chernobyl graphite fire heat release

#### Acids, Bases & pH
- **pH Calculations**: [H‚Å∫], [OH‚Åª], pH = -log[H‚Å∫]
- **Neutralization**: Acid + base ‚Üí salt + water
- **Titration**: Equivalence point, indicators
- **Buffer Solutions**: Resist pH change
- **Example**: Cleopatra's pearl CaCO‚ÇÉ + acetic acid, stomach antacid neutralization, acid rain damage

#### Solutions & Concentration
- **Molarity**: mol/L calculations
- **Dilutions**: M‚ÇÅV‚ÇÅ = M‚ÇÇV‚ÇÇ
- **Percent Composition**: % by mass, % by volume
- **PPM/PPB**: Environmental contamination levels
- **Example**: Nicotine pesticide dilution 80% ‚Üí 0.25%, Flint water Pb¬≤‚Å∫ concentration (15 ppb limit)

#### Gas Laws
- **Ideal Gas Law**: PV = nRT
- **Dalton's Law**: Partial pressures
- **Graham's Law**: Diffusion rates
- **Example**: Apollo 13 CO‚ÇÇ volume calculations, Hindenburg H‚ÇÇ gas volume (200,000 m¬≥)

#### Nuclear Chemistry
- **Radioactive Decay**: Alpha (Œ±), beta (Œ≤), gamma (Œ≥) radiation
- **Half-life**: N(t) = N‚ÇÄ(¬Ω)^(t/t¬Ω)

## üìå Key Success Metrics

A well-designed chemistry task should:

1. **Place student in authentic professional role** (not "you are a student studying...")
2. **Provide specific historical/contemporary context** (exact dates, locations, people, data)
3. **Connect chemistry to real-world consequences** (lives, disasters, mysteries, innovations)
4. **Require curriculum-aligned chemical calculations** (stoichiometry, pH, half-life, etc.)
5. **Integrate interdisciplinary knowledge** (history, space, forensics, art, industry)
6. **Use real chemicals and realistic data** (not made-up scenarios)
7. **Create intellectual engagement** ("Oh! THIS is how chemists use stoichiometry!")
